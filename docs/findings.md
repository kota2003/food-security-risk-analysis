# Global Food Security Risk — Key Findings

**Project:** Global Food Security Risk Analysis  
**Data Coverage:** 200 countries, 2010–2022  
**Analysis Basis:** FAOSTAT + World Bank datasets (2,599 observations)  
**Target Audience:** Policy makers, NGOs, think tank analysts

---

## Executive Summary

Approximately **1 in 10 people worldwide** lacks adequate food access. This analysis identifies the countries at greatest risk, the factors driving that risk, and the structural patterns that make food insecurity so persistent — particularly in Sub-Saharan Africa.

**The central finding is clear:** food insecurity is fundamentally a problem of insufficient caloric supply, compounded by poverty. Countries that cannot feed their populations adequately also tend to be those with the lowest incomes — and breaking this cycle requires addressing both simultaneously.

---

## Finding 1 — The Highest-Risk Countries (2022)

The following countries face the most severe food insecurity risk based on 2022 data:

| Rank | Country | Predicted Risk |
|------|---------|---------------|
| 1 | Haiti | 35.3% |
| 2 | Madagascar | 32.8% |
| 3 | DR Congo | 31.5% |
| 4 | Zambia | 27.1% |
| 5 | Liberia | 26.2% |

> **Note:** Somalia — historically the highest-risk country (52.1% in earlier years) — is absent from the 2022 ranking due to missing data, not improvement.

**What these countries have in common:**
- Critically low daily caloric supply (below 2,000 kcal per person)
- GDP per capita among the lowest in the world
- High dependence on cereal imports (limited domestic food production)

These countries share a common risk profile, suggesting that targeted, coordinated interventions — rather than country-by-country responses — may be more effective.

---

## Finding 2 — Caloric Supply Is the Single Most Powerful Driver

Of all factors analyzed, **daily dietary energy supply (calories per person)** has by far the greatest influence on food insecurity risk — nearly **4× more impact** than the second-ranked factor.

| Driver | Effect | Direction |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| Daily caloric supply (kcal/person) | Very high | ↓ More calories = less risk |
| Protein supply (g/person) | Moderate | ↓ More protein = less risk |
| Cereal import dependency | Low | ↑ More imports = more risk |
| Population growth rate | Low | ↑ Faster growth = more risk |
| Rainfall (precipitation) | Low | ↓ More rain = less risk |
| GDP per capita | Low* | ↓ Higher income = less risk |

*GDP's statistical coefficient appears small because it is closely intertwined with caloric supply (see Finding 3).

**Policy implication:** Programs that increase domestic food production, improve supply chains, or reduce food waste will have the greatest measurable impact on undernourishment rates.

---

## Finding 3 — Food Supply and Poverty Cannot Be Separated

GDP per capita and daily caloric supply are deeply intertwined (correlation: **r = 0.80**). In practical terms: wealthier countries produce and distribute more food; poorer countries cannot.

This means that interventions targeting *either* income growth *or* food supply in isolation will face structural limits. A country's food security is a reflection of its broader economic capacity.

**Policy implication:** Sustainable food security improvements require simultaneous investment in economic development (income, infrastructure) and food systems (agriculture, storage, distribution). Single-track aid programs are unlikely to produce lasting change.

---

## Finding 4 — Africa Is the Only Region Showing No Improvement

Over the 2010–2022 period, most regions of the world saw gradual progress on food security. Africa is the exception.

| Region | Average Risk (2022 model) |
|--------|--------------------------|
| Africa | 15.7% |
| Asia | 8.5% |
| Americas | 7.7% |
| Oceania | 6.9% |
| Europe | 2.9% |

Africa's rate has remained flat at approximately **19%** throughout the entire study period — roughly **2× the global average** and **5× the European rate**. No other region shows this stagnation.

**Policy implication:** Africa requires sustained, long-term commitment from international bodies, not short-term crisis response. The flat trend despite global progress suggests systemic barriers — not temporary shocks — are at work.

---

## Finding 5 — COVID-19 Reversed Years of Progress

The global undernourishment rate declined steadily from approximately 10.3% (2011) to 9.4% (2020). Beginning in 2021, that progress reversed.

The countries hit hardest by this reversal:

| Country | Change (2019 → 2022) |
|---------|----------------------|
| Syria | +10.5 percentage points |
| Kenya | +8.5 percentage points |

This reversal is consistent with documented COVID-19 impacts on global supply chains, agricultural labor, and food distribution systems.

**Policy implication:** Food security systems are fragile in the face of large external shocks. Resilience-building — including local food production capacity and emergency food reserves — reduces vulnerability to future disruptions.

---

## Finding 6 — Political Instability Works Through Economic Channels

Political stability was found to have a near-zero direct statistical relationship with food insecurity. This does **not** mean politics are irrelevant.

Instead, it reflects that political instability typically damages food security *indirectly* — by disrupting GDP growth and destroying food supply infrastructure. The impact is real, but it is already captured in the economic and caloric supply variables.

**Policy implication:** Conflict resolution and governance improvements are food security interventions, even if their effects are not immediately visible in undernourishment statistics.

## Country Risk Clusters

Countries can be grouped into four risk profiles:

| Cluster | Risk Level | Characteristics |
|---------|-----------|----------------|
| High Risk | >20% undernourished | Low caloric supply, low GDP, Sub-Saharan Africa |
| Medium Risk | 10–20% | Moderate food access, developing economies |
| Low Risk | 5–10% | Adequate supply, middle-income countries |
| Very Low Risk | <5% | Strong food systems, high-income countries |

---

## Data Limitations

| Limitation | What It Means |
|------------|--------------|
| FAOSTAT records a minimum of 2.5% | Low-risk countries may appear slightly higher than reality |
| Poverty rate data missing for 60% of observations | A key driver of food insecurity is absent from the model |
| 2022 data unavailable for some countries (e.g., Somalia) | High-risk countries may be underrepresented in recent rankings |
| Statistical association ≠ causation | Coefficients describe patterns, not proven causal mechanisms |

---

## Summary for Decision Makers

1. **Prioritize caloric supply** — it is the most powerful lever available.
2. **Treat food and economic policy as inseparable** — they reinforce each other.
3. **Direct sustained resources to Africa** — other global trends are not reaching this region.
4. **Build resilience, not just response** — COVID demonstrated how quickly progress can reverse.
5. **Invest in data** — missing data for the world's most vulnerable countries undermines effective targeting.
